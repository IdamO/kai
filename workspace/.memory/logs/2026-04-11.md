### Finding 1: ICLR 2025 — "Rethinking Bradley-Terry Models in Preference-Based Reward Modeling"
Paper by Hao Sun et al. (ICLR 2025). Explores preference representation learning that embeds responses into latent spaces to capture intransitive preference structures. Key claim: standard BT models struggle with intransitivity. Alternative: learn preference embeddings in latent space with linear query complexity.
**Why this matters for EXP-R01:** Our ProjDot64 IS a preference embedding in a learned 64d latent space — empirically discovered the same architecture this paper theoretically motivates. Our finding that preference is LINEAR (ProjDot >> MLP) is a strong empirical contribution — the paper focuses on making BT more expressive for intransitivity, but we show for music transitions, simple linear projection works. Open question: does our DJ transition data contain intransitive cycles? If yes → the linear model handles it anyway (interesting), or it doesn't and we need the paper's approach.

### Architecture Decision:
**Production taste vector = 64d from curriculum-trained L24 (CLS).** Not 192d.
- Simplest model, highest min metric
- 65K params (vs 197K for 192d)
- Per-layer neighbors during training (not multi-layer — easier neighbors generalize better)
- The orthogonal multi-layer composition (Phase 3/3b) is geometrically beautiful but operationally redundant once curriculum training teaches generalization
- HOWEVER: multi-layer may still help for specific use cases (context-specific weighting: DJ mode vs playlist mode from Phase 4a). Keep the option.
