The 12 builds, ordered by product magic per engineering day
1. Anchor Selection Model (THE keystone build). Input: user's top-k history embeddings + candidate discovery track. Output: which anchor maximizes P(user accepts discovery | mashup sounds coherent). Loss: contrastive over (anchor, discovery, user) triples mined from DJ transitions where the DJ's next track became a hit in that crowd. Data: 4.44M MixesDB transitions filtered by ListenBrainz follow-through. This is the only model that directly optimizes the Bath Playlist moment. Composes: TransitionScorer v8 (mashup quality) × ProjDot64 L24 (preference) × BridgePredictor (community crossing). This is your #1 build. Everything else is support.
2. Mashup Coherence → Preference Transfer Head. Train a tiny head: given (anchor MERT, discovery MERT, mashup acoustic features), predict whether the listener (not DJ) will skip. Use Last.fm scrobble follow-through after radio transitions (NTS/WFMU/KEXP → 728K transitions) as your label. This separates "DJs think it works" from "humans actually stay." Closes your DJ/listener 27% gap productively.
3. Wormhole Detector. You've theorized them; go find them. Run Louvain on the playlist co-occurrence graph at multiple resolutions, identify tracks with high betweenness across distant communities (not just adjacent ones — that's just bridges). Then train a 64d→1 head on MERT L24 to predict wormhole-ness. Hypothesis: wormholes have high spectral centroid variance + low semantic typicality (L24 CLS far from any cluster centroid). If r > 0.6 you have a publishable finding and a product feature: "the agent occasionally sends you a wormhole" — discovery that should not work but does. This is your "holy shit" candidate. [70% it replicates]
4. Cold Start via Mashup Triangulation. New user picks 3 songs they love. Don't fit a preference vector — instead, generate 5 mashups pairing each seed with candidates from different 64d regions. Their accept/skip pattern on 5 mashups localizes them in preference space faster than 50 listens, because mashups are designed discriminative probes. Active learning via D-optimal design over the 64d basis. You go from zero to a usable preference vector in ~90 seconds of interaction. No profile, no questions — just "does this sound good."
5. Behavioral Signal → Preference Update (no catastrophic forgetting). Don't retrain. Maintain the 64d vector as an EMA in the rotated basis you already discovered. Skip = negative gradient along the discovery's projection; full-listen = positive. Because the basis is orthogonal and preference is linear, EMA updates are provably stable — this is the gift of your linearity finding. Add a slow drift term toward the user's L5 (acoustic) centroid to prevent semantic-only collapse.
6. The Gift Bridge Primitive. Cancer shell solution: when A gifts to B, never expose A's vector. Instead compute the midpoint track in 64d between A's recent listening centroid and B's — find the real track closest to that midpoint, send that. Both users experience "this is for us" without either's taste being legible. The midpoint operation is the entire information flow. Network effect: every gift creates a new edge in a private affinity graph you can mine for community structure without ever showing it to users.
7. Curator Residual Model. Train a 27d model (the DJ-specific residual) per active curator on MixesDB. Now you can offer "the agent today is channeling [unnamed DJ X]" without naming them — users feel a coherent personality shift week to week. The weekly crate gets a secret guest curator. This is how you make the crate feel alive instead of algorithmic.
8. MERT Layer Routing Network. You know L5=acoustic, L24=semantic. Train a gating model: per user, learn the optimal layer mixture (softmax over 25 layers). Some users are L18 people. Nobody knows this exists because nobody has all-layer MERT. Instant personalization axis with zero new data.
9. Temporal Taste Drift Model. 9.9M playlists have timestamps. Fit a per-user trajectory in 64d space and forecast where they're going. Send them tracks 2-4 weeks ahead of their drift vector. They'll feel the agent "saw it coming." Validate against held-out future listens.
10. Anti-Friendship-Paradox Router. Penalize hub tracks at recommendation time by their global degree in the playlist graph raised to some power α. Tune α per user via their tolerance for niche (measured by their own listening graph's clustering coefficient). The agent learns how niche each user wants to go.
11. Mashup Generation Conditioned on Anchor. Given the chosen anchor + discovery, generate the actual 15s audio. You probably need a small diffusion or flow-matching model conditioned on both MERT embeddings + a beat-grid alignment from your 13 audio features. This is the highest-engineering item but it's the product itself — without it you're a recommender, with it you're an agent.
12. Playlist Phase Transition Detector. On the 1.7B playlist-track graph, look for percolation thresholds — tracks that, when added to a community, cause it to merge with another. This is genre emergence in real time. Publishable, and it tells you which tracks to surface before they become hubs. Early-mover discovery engine.
The reframe
Your real question isn't "which models next." It's: what's the smallest set of capabilities that turns the mashup moment into a closed feedback loop? Anchor selection (#1) + mashup generation (#11) + behavioral EMA update (#5) is the loop. Everything else is enrichment of that loop. Build the loop end-to-end at low quality in 2 weeks, then improve each component independently — that's how you stay in research mode without losing the product thread. Your linearity finding is the secret weapon: it means you can update, compose, and interpolate preference vectors with linear algebra, which means the loop is cheap to iterate on.
The "holy shit" finding to chase: wormholes have a detectable audio signature in MERT layer X. If true, you've discovered that the structure of music discovery is encoded in the audio itself, not just in social co-listening. That's a Nature Human Behaviour paper and a product feature simultaneously.
